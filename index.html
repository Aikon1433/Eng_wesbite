Safer Cycling in San Luis Obispo

Cycling, walking, and other forms of micromobility are central to how people move around San Luis Obispo and the Cal Poly campus. Students, faculty, and local residents use bikes, e-bikes, scooters, and walking routes every day to reach classes, housing, jobs, and downtown destinations. These modes are often faster than walking, more convenient than driving for short trips, and essential for people who do not own a car.

However, many of the paths and streets they rely on were never designed for such a high volume of riders and pedestrians using the same space. Narrow painted bike lanes, shared routes, and crowded campus walkways regularly force people into close interaction, which can lead to near misses and serious collisions. Nationally, the United States sees far higher cycling fatality rates than countries like the Netherlands, Denmark, and Germany, where protected lanes and separated paths are standard.

San Luis Obispo reflects these broader trends. In 2014, the city reported an unusually high rate of bicycle collisions for its size, and many more minor crashes and close calls likely go unreported. The purpose of this project is to show that improving bicycle infrastructure in SLO is not just a lifestyle choice—it is a matter of safety, equity, and accessibility for everyone who depends on affordable, sustainable transportation.

What This Site Covers

This proposal:

  • Explains the safety and equity issues riders and pedestrians face.
  • Analyzes collision data, survey results, and on-the-ground conditions.
  • Outlines concrete, cost-effective infrastructure solutions.
  • Provides research and references for policymakers and community members.

Our Goal

To prove that current conditions in SLO and on the Cal Poly campus are not safe for cyclists, and to offer practical, financially realistic steps toward a protected, predictable network that works better for everyone who walks, rides, or drives through the city.
