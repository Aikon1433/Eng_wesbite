Conclusions & Further Research

Although our findings show that rebuilding the most dangerous segment of campus as a Class I bikeway could cost approximately $170,000—a relatively small price—the fact that this investment has not yet been made raises a larger question: what are policymakers missing? In official data, most crashes go unreported as the people affected often think filing a report is not worth the hassle, if they think of it at all (Medury et al., 2019). Campus-based research using crowdsourced surveys has found that the majority of pedestrian and bicycle crashes around universities never make it into police reports, especially when injuries are minor or there is little property damage (Medury et al., 2019).

Riders often decide it is “not worth the hassle” to call the police for a fall, a low-speed crash with another bike, or a collision with a friend. Others may worry about getting themselves in trouble if they were riding without lights, breaking a traffic rule, or using campus spaces in technically “wrong” ways, so they simply walk it off instead of making a formal report. Many incidents also happen on paths, parking lots, and other campus property where no officer ever shows up, so there is no automatic record at all (Medury et al., 2019). By reporting even minor crashes, however, lawmakers and city planners can gain the visibility they need to develop solutions and prevent more serious crashes in the future.

Non-motorized travel is usually measured poorly, with walking and cycling lumped together and treated as a side note to vehicular traffic. As one review of bicycle research puts it, the “black cloud looming over all analysis of non-motorized transportation” is the lack of good data and the tendency to treat cycling as marginal (Pucher & Buehler, 2016). When near-misses and “small” crashes disappear into this data gap, it creates a false sense of safety. Only a handful of official crashes are considered by policymakers who may conclude that the system works fine, while students deal with daily close calls and minor crashes that go unreported and unsolved (Medury et al., 2019).

Research on bike facilities suggests that projects like a separated path near Cal Poly should be seen less as a one-time expense and more as a long-term public investment. Kevin J. Krizec’s review of economic studies on bicycling facilities shows that, in the small number of cases where full benefit–cost ratios have been calculated, benefits consistently exceed costs—often by several multiples—especially in places that have not yet invested heavily in cycling infrastructure (Krizec, 2007). This pattern appears in very different contexts, from European and Latin American cities to Norwegian towns building walking and cycling networks, suggesting that the basic economic logic of bike investments is robust across regions (Krizec, 2007).

Krizec also notes that the benefits of bike infrastructure are not limited to faster or more convenient trips. He points to a broad set of gains, including improved mobility, increased physical activity, health-care savings, enhanced cyclist safety, reduced congestion and pollution, and overall improvements in neighborhood livability (Krizec, 2007). The health aspect alone is striking: across the studies he reviewed, estimated annual health-care cost savings from physical activity range from around $19 to $1,175 per person, with a median of $128 per person per year (Krizec, 2007). If a protected campus route encouraged even a few hundred students to bicycle regularly, the long-term health savings, combined with avoided crash costs and other benefits, would make a $170,000 path look small when spread across its lifetime.

The next steps are clear. Cal Poly and city partners should create a simple reporting system for crashes and near misses near campus. A web map or app would allow riders and pedestrians to flag hotspots in real time (Medury et al., 2019). Engineers would gain a sharper view of problem areas instead of relying on sparse police records. This information should guide staged construction of a protected network. The first priority should focus on campus entrances and the promenade segment already highlighted in this proposal, with later phases extending protection along major commuter streets such as Foothill, California, and Monterey (California Department of Transportation, 2020).

Investments in safe cycling infrastructure are not a luxury. Students who depend on bikes, e-bikes, scooters, and other small forms of transportation need safer routes that reduce the risk of injury and make it easier to reach classes and jobs. Clear separation and predictable movements keep stress and conflict to a minimum for pedestrians as well. The broader community will see fewer car trips and fewer crashes, easing pressure on streets, budgets, and emergency services (Pucher & Buehler, 2016; Krizec, 2007). An investment of approximately $170,000 in protected segments on campus and city routes would move SLO toward a safer, more equitable, and healthier future.
